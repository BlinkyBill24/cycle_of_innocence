# Cycle of Innocence — Story Bible

**Working Title**: Cycle of Innocence  
**Genre/Tone**: 2D top-down action-adventure RPG. Blends the wonder and exploration of classic Zelda/Secret of Mana with the creeping dread, body horror, and psychological tension of Super Metroid, Silent Hill, and Alien. Conspiracy layers and escalating revelations in the style of Attack on Titan. Deep coming-of-age and life-sim progression like Fable, but filtered through horror and moral consequence.  
**Core Theme**: You were never meant to grow up. The world requires children to die so that adults can pretend to be safe. Your escape breaks the pattern — and the pattern will do anything to correct itself. The only creatures that ever showed you mercy were animals. The only family you will ever have might be the ones you raise... or corrupt.

**Length Target (v1)**: 6–10 hours focused campaign. High replayability through morality, companion fates, and 4 major endings + NG+ knowledge carry-over.

## Protagonist: Rowan

**True Name / "Vessel" Designation**: Subject-07 (the seventh "perfect" candidate in the current cycle).

**Background (The Escape)**:
Rowan is 9 years old at the start. They were selected as this generation's "Offering" during the Night of the Hollowing — a secret ritual held every 7–12 years when the "Hunger" beneath the village grows restless. Selection is random, like a lottery or based on a "Community Harmony Score" that parents (and families) accumulate through work, loyalty displays, volunteering, and other contributions to village life. High-scoring or "lucky" families are "honored" by having a child chosen. The ritual takes place at the village playground — a place children are supposed to feel safe and happy — at dusk, with colorful but slightly wrong decorations.

The "Playtime Guardians" (creepy clowns in bright but faded costumes, oversized stuffed animals with too many stitches, and living toys that move on their own) lead the chosen child through gentle games and songs while the elders chant softly. Something ancient "tastes" the offering through the toys and laughter that turns hollow.

This time, something went "wrong" (or right, depending on perspective):
- One of the handlers (a conflicted young "Clown" performer named Thorne, wearing a cracked smiling mask) hesitated during the final "hug" phase and loosened the toy restraints.
- A sudden unnatural fog or "mistake" in the rite (the toys stopped singing) allowed Rowan to slip away into the growing dark.
- As Rowan fled the playground, the village carried on as if nothing had happened — they believed the ritual had been successful. No one raised an alarm. The adults smiled and went home thinking "the Hunger is fed, all is well."
- In the chaos at the edge of the playground, Rowan found (or was found by) a small terrified hound pup that had also been brought as part of the "lesser offering." The pup's mother had been killed earlier that day. The two escaped together into the fringes.

This is the inciting incident. Rowan is now a living crack in the cycle. The village does not yet realize the escape happened. The Hunger was not satisfied. Over the following days and weeks, strange things begin to happen (crops wither in patches, the dead do not stay quiet, "monsters" appear more frequently). The villagers start to wonder why the Offering "failed" and begin escalating the lottery — sacrificing more and more children in frantic additional rituals. This creates even more monsters from the new failures, accelerating the horror.

Rowan, hiding in the fringes with Briar, begins to piece together that the village still thinks everything is fine… for now.

**Age Progression & Visual/Moral Changes** (tied to [[characters/protagonist]]):

- **Child (9–11)**: Small, big haunted eyes, simple rough tunic with ritual tear marks. Innocent but already carrying guilt for surviving when others didn't. Movement is light and quick but vulnerable.
- **Early Teen (13–15)**: Taller, leaner, eyes harder. Starts wearing scavenged cloaks or pieces of old "ward" symbols. Can choose to keep small kindnesses (flowers for graves, sharing food with animals) or become colder.
- **Late Teen / Young Adult (18–22)**: Full height, posture changes with morality. High kindness = still some light in the eyes, protective stance. High ruthlessness = scars (self-inflicted or from using companions as weapons), "marked" glowing veins or shadow under skin that animals react to.

**Morality System Impact**:
Morality is not simple good/evil. It is "How much of yourself are you willing to sacrifice to survive... and what are you willing to sacrifice others for?"

- Positive (Kind / Empath): Rowan tries to protect the innocent (including animals and the few humans who show doubt). Animals bond deeply and gain abilities. Some "monsters" can be calmed or even redeemed. Revelations hit harder emotionally.
- Neutral (Pragmatic): Survival first, but not cruel. Bonds are functional. Can still achieve the "best" endings but with more loss.
- Negative (Ruthless / Vessel): Rowan uses people and animals as tools. Faster combat power (companions can be forced into dangerous roles). Body horror spreads earlier to Rowan and companions. Darker dialogue options and unique "corrupted" abilities. High chance of tragic or horror endings for the animals.

Key early choice example: In the first village edge area, Rowan can steal food from a desperate family or leave some of their own scavenged supplies. This sets early morality and affects whether a later NPC recognizes them as "the one who showed mercy" or "the thief."

## The Cycle & The Hunger (Core Conspiracy)

The village (called "Hollowmere" or "The Still Village" in some tellings) has a pact older than memory.

Every few years, one or more children are selected via the Harmony Lottery (or low family score) and offered during the Night of the Hollowing at the village playground. The ritual feeds and calms an ancient entity buried deep beneath the land — "The Hunger" or "The First Hollow."

In exchange, the village is "protected":
- Crops grow (twisted but abundant).
- The dead stay quiet.
- Outsiders get lost in the woods or are driven mad before they can reach the village.
- The "monsters" (corrupted previous offerings) mostly stay in the deep woods and only occasionally take someone who wanders too far.

**Major Twists** (layered revelations that should feel like punches):

1. **The Monsters Are Us**: The "beasts" and "spirits" that haunt the woods and occasionally raid are the failed or "completed" previous sacrifices. Their bodies were changed by the Hunger. Their minds are fractured echoes of the children they once were. Some still clutch broken toys or wear faded clown ruffs from the playground ritual.

2. **The Elders Are the Previous Cycle**: Many of the current elders and "protectors" are not the original wardens. They are survivors or "successful" offerings from 20–40 years ago who were allowed (or forced) to return in twisted, aged forms after "serving their time." They perpetuate the cycle because they believe (or have been broken into believing) that it is the only way to keep the Hunger from consuming *everything*.

3. **Rowan Was Never Random**: Rowan's bloodline carries a rare trait — they are a "perfect vessel." One of their ancestors was the child who *almost* broke the cycle generations ago but was recaptured and used to strengthen the seal instead. The Hunger has been waiting specifically for someone like Rowan. The "mistake" in the ritual may not have been a mistake at all — it may have been the Hunger *letting* them escape so it could hunt a stronger meal later.

4. **The Animals Are Also Part of the Cycle**: The companions Rowan raises were never ordinary. Briar (the dog) and the others carry echoes of previous companions from past escaped children. The Hunger corrupts bonds. If Rowan is ruthless, the animals can become new vessels or weapons for the cycle. If Rowan is kind, they can become the key to truly breaking or transforming it. The final twist can involve one of the animals having to be sacrificed or choosing to sacrifice themselves.

**The "Protectors" Lie**: The elders tell the village that the offerings keep the monsters away and the land fertile. In truth, the cycle *creates* the monsters and the land's "fertility" is the Hunger slowly digesting the world from below.

## Animal Companions (Found Family & Horror)

These are not pets. They are the only real family Rowan will ever have. Their arcs are as important as Rowan's.

**Briar (The Hound — First Companion, Act 0 Escape)**:
- Rescued as a terrified, half-starved pup during the escape.
- Abilities: Tracking (find hidden paths or lies), digging (reveal buried truths or bodies), loyal melee assist that grows stronger with bond.
- Arc: Briar is the emotional heart. High kindness = Briar becomes brave and protective, even willing to face the Hunger. Low morality = Briar is used as a weapon, becomes aggressive, and can be corrupted into a monstrous version that Rowan may have to put down or that turns on the village (tragic or horrifying).
- Key choice: Early on, Rowan can share their tiny amount of food with Briar or keep it all for themselves when both are starving.

**Echo (The Bird — Mid Game, Act 1)**:
- A sharp-eyed raven or unusual raptor that starts following Rowan after they show mercy (or ruthlessness) in the woods.
- Abilities: Scouting ahead (warning of danger or revealing distant events), carrying small objects, distracting enemies, aerial puzzle solutions (dropping items on switches).
- More fragile. Loss or corruption hits very hard narratively.
- Arc: Echo represents knowledge and warning. It can overhear elder conversations or see things from above that force revelations earlier.

**Storm / Ash (The Mount — Late Game, Act 2)**:
- A sturdy horse or stag-like creature (perhaps a "wild" one that was also marked or escaped a different ritual).
- Abilities: Faster travel between zones, powerful charge that can break obstacles or enemies, can carry Rowan + supplies, provides a "safe" feeling when resting.
- Requires the most care. Neglect or using it in horror events can break the bond fastest.
- Arc: Becomes a symbol of freedom or a tragic vessel in the climax.

**Corruption & Bond Mechanics** (mechanical + narrative):
- Bond score per companion (tracked in PlayerData and dialogue variables: $bond_briar, $corruption_briar).
- Care actions (feed, soothe after dread events, protect in combat) increase bond.
- Using them selfishly or exposing them to the Hunger increases corruption.
- High corruption on a companion can unlock powerful but horrifying "corrupted abilities" at the cost of loyalty and story consequences (they may refuse commands, attack Rowan, or become new horror set pieces).
- In endings, companions can survive together with Rowan, be lost heroically, be corrupted and have to be stopped, or even "ascend" with Rowan in a transformed cycle.

## High-Level Act Structure (Child → Adult)

**Act 0: The Escape (Child, ~9–10) — "The Night Everything Broke"**
- Opening: The ritual at the playground (with creepy clowns and living toys). Failure due to Thorne's hesitation + the fog. Flight with Briar into the night.
- First zone: The playground at dusk turning to night, then the immediate fringes (horror tutorial — bright colors turning wrong, toy sounds becoming threatening, limited visibility as fog rolls in, first glimpse of a "monster" that still has a stuffed animal in its hand).
- Rowan hides near the village edge. The adults are smiling and going about their evening as if the ritual succeeded perfectly. No one is looking for Rowan. The village believes "the Offering was accepted, the Hunger is calm, all is well."
- First morality choice + first real bond moment with Briar (while hiding and overhearing normal village life).
- Strange things begin happening in the background (a crop patch dies overnight, a dog howls wrong). The villagers start whispering that maybe the lottery needs to be run again soon.
- Ends with Rowan and Briar forced to go on the run or hide in the fringes as the first extra "emergency" offerings begin. First hint that the village's "success" is a lie that is already cracking.

**Act 1: The Fringes (Early Teen, ~13–15) — "We Only Have Each Other"**
- Wider exploration of the cursed woods and abandoned outlying farms.
- Recruitment / meeting of second companion (Echo the bird).
- First "hunt" — either Rowan is hunted by village "wardens" or they hunt a monster together.
- Major early twist: Discovery of old ritual remains or a previous child's belongings that prove the cycle has happened before.
- Morality branches open: How do you treat the few humans you meet (a doubting young warden, a mad hermit who was once an offering)?
- Companion raising mechanics introduced properly.
- Ends with a larger revelation or a direct confrontation that forces Rowan to leave the immediate area and go deeper (or return to the village in disguise).

**Act 2: The Heart of the Lie (Late Teen, ~17–18) — "Everyone Was Lying"**
- Return to or infiltration of Hollowmere and the deeper ritual sites.
- Major twists 2 & 3 land (monsters = previous children, elders = previous "successes").
- Third companion (the mount) and loyalty crises (one companion may be badly corrupted or captured depending on choices).
- Body horror escalates — Rowan or companions start showing physical changes based on morality and exposure.
- Found family vs blood family conflict (possible living relatives who either try to "save" Rowan by completing the ritual or reveal they always knew).
- Climactic choice point that determines the final path.

**Act 3: The Hollowing (Young Adult, 20+) — "What Will You Become?"**
- The deepest ritual site / the Hunger itself.
- Full truth about Rowan's bloodline and the animals' true nature.
- Final confrontations with the current "wardens" and possibly a corrupted version of a previous companion or even a twisted elder who was once kind.
- The choice: Break it (costly), Transform it (risky hope), Perpetuate it (power at the price of everything), or be broken by it.
- Multiple endings with different fates for Rowan and each companion.
- Epilogue that changes based on morality, which companions lived, and what was revealed.

# The Four Major Endings

There are four primary ending paths, each with variations based on which companions lived, how corrupted they (and Rowan) became, and what revelations were known. NG+ carries over morality, known revelations, and companion “echo” states for new dialogue and slightly altered events.

All endings should feel earned and thematically consistent. Kind playthroughs are bittersweet and hopeful in a quiet way. Ruthless playthroughs are powerful but hollow and often horrifying.

## Ending 1: The Breaking (Sacrifice to End It)

**Requirements** (approximate):
- At least one companion must have high bond and low corruption.
- Rowan must have rejected the “vessel” power or used it against the Hunger rather than for personal survival.
- The final ritual site must have been fully understood (major revelations known).

**What Happens**:
Rowan and their remaining bonded companions enter the deepest chamber. They use Rowan’s blood (as the perfect vessel) combined with the “pure” bond of the animal(s) to create a true seal. The Hunger is not destroyed — it is put into a true sleep that will last far longer than any previous cycle, possibly forever.

**Variations**:
- **Rowan dies / is changed forever**: The most common version. Rowan’s body is the price. They may live long enough to see the sun rise with their surviving companions before fading. Bittersweet and emotional.
- **A companion chooses to share the burden**: One animal (usually Briar) stays with Rowan in the seal. The others escape and are seen in the epilogue living free.
- **All companions survive but Rowan is left broken**: Possible with very high bonds across the board. Rowan lives but is physically and spiritually damaged. The final image is of the animals staying with the ruined Rowan anyway.

**Tone**: Tragic heroism. Love and found family win, but at terrible cost. The village will eventually forget or rewrite what happened.

**Epilogue**: The animals (or their descendants) are seen generations later, still carrying small tokens Rowan gave them. The Hunger is quiet. No more offerings.

## Ending 2: The Transformation (Change the Pact)

**Requirements**:
- High overall bond with at least two companions.
- Rowan has learned the full truth about the animals also being “marked” by previous cycles.
- Rowan chooses the difficult path of trying to renegotiate with the Hunger rather than feed it or destroy the vessel.

**What Happens**:
Using the unique nature of the companions (creatures that were *meant* to be part of the cycle but were instead loved and raised outside it), Rowan offers a new pact. The Hunger is not sealed or fed children — it is given the animals’ bonds as a different kind of sustenance. In exchange, it changes. The “monsters” of the old cycle are released or transformed. The land begins to heal in strange, wild ways.

**Variations**:
- **The companions ascend / change with the Hunger**: They become something new — guardians or spirits of the new order. Bittersweet; they are no longer ordinary animals, but they are not suffering.
- **One companion stays with Rowan in the new world**: The most hopeful version. Rowan and one (or more) animals survive as strange, changed beings who watch over the breaking of the old village.
- **The transformation is imperfect**: Some of the old cycle’s monsters remain. The village is still afraid. But no more children are taken.

**Tone**: Hopeful but alien. The world is no longer the same, and the price is that nothing returns to “normal.” Found family literally reshapes the rules of the world.

**Epilogue**: Years later, strange new growth appears where the old ritual sites were. A dog, a bird, and a horse (or their spiritual echoes) are sometimes seen together with a tall, scarred figure at the edge of the woods. The cycle is broken, but something new and wild has taken its place.

## Ending 3: The Perpetuation (Become the New Warden)

**Requirements**:
- High corruption on Rowan and/or at least one companion.
- Rowan accepts the vessel power and uses the corrupted strength of their bonds.
- The player has leaned into ruthless or pragmatic choices.

**What Happens**:
Rowan takes the elders’ place. They become the new keeper of the cycle, but with their animals as a twisted “pack.” The Hunger is fed, but Rowan has more control than previous wardens. They can choose who is taken and when. The village fears them even more than the old elders.

**Variations**:
- **Lonely tyrant**: All companions are dead or fully corrupted into monstrous servants. Rowan rules alone, powerful and empty.
- **Pack of monsters**: Rowan + one or two corrupted companions become the new horror that stalks the woods. Other “monsters” from previous cycles now answer to them. Genuinely terrifying ending.
- **“Merciful” warden**: Rowan still takes offerings but tries to make it “kinder” (only the cruel, only adults who volunteer, etc.). Still horrific, but with a twisted sense of mercy. Some companions stay with them in this dark new order.

**Tone**: Horror power fantasy. You win, in the worst possible way. The player should feel the weight of what they became.

**Epilogue**: The village is “safe.” Offerings continue, but now the thing in the woods sometimes wears familiar scars or has a dog that used to be loved. Rowan (or what’s left of them) watches from the trees.

## Ending 4: The Failure / Embrace (The Cycle Claims Everything)

**Requirements**:
- Very high corruption + low bonds (or all companions dead/corrupted).
- Rowan tries to break the cycle but is too broken or too alone to succeed.
- Or Rowan fully embraces the Hunger without trying to control it.

**What Happens**:
Rowan is consumed or transformed. They become one of the new “monsters” — or the new perfect vessel that strengthens the cycle for another generation. In the worst versions, their corrupted companions become the new hunters that drag future children into the circle.

**Variations**:
- **Rowan becomes the thing in the woods**: Classic body/identity horror. The final image is something that still has Rowan’s eyes (or Briar’s) watching the next offering.
- **Total loss**: Everyone dies or is taken. The village continues exactly as before, perhaps telling stories of the “wicked child who tried to run.”
- **Pyrrhic escape**: Rowan gets away alone, all companions lost or turned. They live, but broken and haunted, knowing the cycle continues without them. The bleakest “survival” ending.

**Tone**: Despair and horror. The cycle wins. Your choices mattered — they just weren’t enough.

**Epilogue**: The Night of the Hollowing continues. Somewhere, another child is being dressed in white. Maybe, far away, a lone scarred figure watches and does nothing.

## Ending Flags Quick Reference

The game tracks:
- Rowan’s final morality tier
- Which companions are alive + their final bond/corruption state
- Which major revelations were known before the final choice
- Whether Rowan accepted, rejected, or tried to transform the vessel power

These flags are checked in the final dialogue nodes and the epilogue sequences.

**Best Ending** (most players will feel is “good”): Transformation path with at least two companions surviving with high bond and low corruption.

**“I became a monster” Ending**: Perpetuation with corrupted companions.

**Tragic Hero Ending**: Breaking the cycle at the cost of Rowan and/or one or more companions.

**Total Loss**: Failure path.

See the full choice matrix for the specific decisions that move these flags.

This is the emotional payoff the entire game has been building toward. Every companion bond scene, every morality choice, and every revelation should make these endings feel personal and earned.

# Major Choices & Consequences Matrix

This is the living document of the most important player decisions. Each entry includes:
- When it happens (Act / rough location)
- The choice
- Immediate effects
- Long-term effects on morality, companions, revelations, and endings
- dialogue variable changes

**Design Goal**: Every meaningful choice should affect at least two of: companion bond/corruption, available dialogue/revelations, world reactivity, and ending eligibility. No “pure flavor” choices in the critical path.

## Early Game (Act 0 — The Escape & First Night)

### The Food
- **Choice**: Rowan has a tiny amount of scavenged hard bread and dried meat after fleeing. Briar (the pup) is starving and whimpering.
  - **Share with Briar**: +15 bond_briar, small negative to Rowan’s current_hp (hunger). Rowan says something kind.
  - **Keep for yourself**: +5 corruption_briar (Briar learns Rowan is not safe). Rowan stays at full hunger but feels guilty (or doesn’t).
- **Long-term**: This is the very first signal to the system about Rowan’s nature. High bond here makes Briar more likely to stand his ground in later fear events. Low bond makes him more likely to cower or eventually turn.

### The First “Monster” (Optional Mercy)
- **Choice**: Rowan and Briar encounter a shambling, half-formed thing wearing the remnants of a child’s white ritual robe. It is in pain and not immediately aggressive.
  - **Mercy kill it quickly**: Neutral to slightly negative morality. Briar is frightened but the thing doesn’t suffer.
  - **Try to help / talk to it**: High positive morality shift. Possible early revelation (it speaks a name or fragment of the cycle). High risk — it may attack in confusion.
  - **Leave it to suffer**: Negative morality. Briar’s bond drops (he doesn’t understand why Rowan would be cruel).
- **Long-term**: If helped and it survives the encounter in some form, it can become a recurring tragic figure or even a late-game ally/horror depending on later choices.

## Act 1 — The Fringes

### The Desperate Family (or Lone Survivor)
- **Choice**: At the edge of an abandoned farm, Rowan encounters a small family (or single parent + child) who are also hiding from the village. They have almost no food.
  - **Steal from them**: +corruption, possible combat or chase if caught. Short-term resource gain. Later: the family (or rumors of them) can turn the village more hostile or create a tragic echo if they are later taken as “replacements.”
  - **Share what little you have**: +morality, bond boost with any companion present. The family may give a small token or warning that becomes useful later.
  - **Drive them away violently**: Strong negative morality. High chance of Briar or Echo reacting badly. Creates a “Rowan the monster” rumor that follows you.
- **Companion reactivity**: Briar will remember if you were kind to the child. Echo may later bring news of what happened to that family.

### The Young Warden (Thorne or equivalent)
- **Choice**: One of the handlers from the original ritual (the one who hesitated) finds Rowan. He claims he wants to help but is clearly terrified and conflicted.
  - **Trust him / work with him**: Opens a path of partial human alliance. He can provide information about the elders and the next ritual. High risk of betrayal or him being captured and used against Rowan.
  - **Kill him / drive him off**: Prevents future betrayal but loses a source of inside knowledge. Strong negative morality hit. Companions may question Rowan.
  - **Use him then discard him**: Worst of both — information + high corruption + later guilt scenes or companion disapproval.
- **Twist potential**: This character can be the one who reveals early that “the monsters used to be like you.”

## Act 2 — Return & Deeper Truths

### The Captured “Monster” (Previous Child)
- **Choice**: Rowan discovers one of the “monsters” that is still somewhat lucid — a teenage girl who was taken two cycles ago. She is chained and being used by the elders to lure new offerings.
  - **Free her and try to help her escape**: Very high positive morality. She may join as a temporary tragic ally or give Rowan critical knowledge before dying. Strong positive effect on companion bonds (especially if Briar interacts with her).
  - **Mercy kill her**: Sad but “practical.” Moderate negative morality. Briar especially will be upset.
  - **Leave her / use her as distraction**: High corruption. She may curse Rowan with her last breath, causing later nightmare or dread events. Echo may refuse to help for a while.
- **Major revelation trigger**: Talking to her (or examining her remains) can unlock the “monsters are previous children” twist early.

### Companion Crisis Point
- **Choice**: One of the companions (most likely Briar or the future mount) is badly injured or captured during a raid on a ritual site.
  - **Risk everything to save them**: Huge bond boost. Possible permanent injury to the companion or Rowan. Opens “redemption of the corrupted” paths later.
  - **Sacrifice the companion to escape / complete the objective**: Massive corruption on that companion (or death). Other companions’ bonds drop hard. Unlocks unique ruthless abilities but poisons later dialogue.
  - **Abandon them**: The companion may survive in a corrupted state and become a recurring antagonist or tragic encounter.

## Act 3 — The Final Choices (Climax)

These are the big ones that directly gate endings.

### The Vessel Revelation
When Rowan learns they are the “perfect vessel”:
- Accept the power offered by the Hunger (to “save” the village on your terms): Strong negative morality. Massive power spike. High chance of companions being corrupted or lost.
- Reject it and try to destroy the vessel within yourself: High positive. Requires help from bonded companions. Very difficult combat or puzzle sequence.
- Offer one of your companions as a substitute vessel: Extremely high corruption for that companion. Possible unique “I did this for you” scenes. Opens some of the darkest endings.

### The Final Stand — Who Pays?
In the deepest ritual chamber, Rowan must decide how to deal with the Hunger:
- Use Rowan’s own blood + a willing (or forced) companion to try to break the cycle permanently.
- Attempt to transform the pact (using the animals’ unique “marked but not broken” nature).
- Take the elders’ place and become the new warden (with corrupted companions as your “pack”).
- Fail / be claimed (horror ending where the cycle continues with Rowan as the new monster).

Each major companion has specific “death / sacrifice / survival / corruption” flags that are checked here. Some endings are locked if too many companions are dead or fully corrupted.

| Choice Type          | Kindness Path Effect                  | Ruthless Path Effect                     | Companion Most Affected |

# Animal Companions — Character Bible & Arcs

These are not sidekicks. They are the emotional core and mechanical heart of *Cycle of Innocence*. Rowan’s only real family. Their survival, loyalty, growth, and possible corruption are as important as Rowan’s own arc.

All companions have:
- Growth stages (tied to Rowan’s age + time spent together)
- Bond score (kindness, care, shared danger)
- Corruption score (ruthless use, exposure to the Hunger, Rowan’s own moral decay)
- Unique abilities that evolve (or twist) with bond/corruption
- Deep narrative presence in dialogue (they react to revelations, can refuse commands, have their own “breaking points”)

## Briar — The Hound (First Companion)

**Rescue**: Act 0, during the failed ritual at the playground / escape. A small, half-starved pup that was also brought as a “lesser offering.” His mother was killed earlier that day as part of the preparations. Rowan and Briar flee together from the playground into the night fringes. The village does not immediately notice because they believe the main ritual succeeded.

**Personality**: Loyal to a fault, initially terrified of everything (including Rowan at first). With positive bond he becomes brave, almost noble. With negative treatment he becomes mean, reactive, and eventually monstrous.

**Appearance Progression**:
- Pup: Scruffy, one ear torn, big fearful eyes, ritual nick on flank.
- Adult (with Rowan): Strong, intelligent working dog. Scars from protecting Rowan.
- Corrupted: Matted fur, glowing wrong-colored eyes, exposed “veins” of Hunger-stuff, hunched and aggressive. Can become a horror encounter if Rowan pushes too far.

**Abilities** (evolve with bond):
- Tracking (scent for hidden paths, buried bodies, or lies told by humans)
- Digging (reveal old ritual sites, escape tunnels, or evidence of previous cycles)
- Melee assist (grows from distracting enemies to powerful takedowns)
- “Guardian Howl” (late game morale boost or fear effect on lesser horrors)

**Bond / Corruption Mechanics**:
- Early game: Sharing food or comforting Briar during the first horror night has huge bond impact.
- Mid game: Forcing Briar to fight when he’s scared or using him to threaten humans increases corruption fast.
- High corruption ending possibilities: Briar turns on Rowan during a critical moment, or Rowan has to mercy-kill a fully corrupted Briar (devastating scene).

**Key Dialogue Scenes**:
- The first night in the woods (bonding or survival choice).
- Briar recognizing the scent of a previous “monster” that used to be a child he somehow remembers.
- Climax choice: Use Briar as a living weapon against the Hunger or protect him even if it costs the “easy” path.

**Thematic Role**: Innocence that can be preserved or weaponized. The purest found-family bond. The one that hurts the most to lose or corrupt.

## Echo — The Bird (Second Companion)

**Rescue / Meeting**: Act 1. After Rowan shows either notable kindness (leaving food for scavengers) or notable ruthlessness (killing something in a particularly cruel way), a sharp-eyed raven (or unusual pale raptor) begins following them. It may have been watching the village rituals for a long time.

**Personality**: Wary, intelligent, a little cruel in its own bird way. It speaks in fragments and warnings. It is the companion most likely to deliver uncomfortable truths.

**Appearance Progression**:
- Starts small and ragged.
- Grows sleeker and more striking.
- Corrupted version: Feathers fall out in patches, eyes become multiple or wrong, it starts bringing “gifts” that are pieces of previous victims.

**Abilities**:
- Aerial scouting (reveals map information, ambushes, or distant events)
- Carrying small items between areas (key for some puzzles)
- Distraction / harassment (can draw enemies away or interrupt casting)
- “Witness” — in certain scenes Echo can have seen or heard things that force revelations even if Rowan tries to avoid them.

**Bond / Corruption**:
- Echo respects competence and honesty. Lying to it or being pointlessly cruel drops bond fast.
- It is the most likely companion to leave if bond becomes too negative (flying away with a final harsh truth).

**Thematic Role**: Knowledge that cannot be unlearned. The cost of seeing too much. A companion that can be lost through emotional neglect as much as physical danger.

## Storm (or Ash) — The Mount (Third Companion)

**Rescue / Meeting**: Act 2. A powerful but haunted horse (or stag-like creature with old “ward” brands) that was once used in rituals or escaped a different part of the cycle. It may have carried previous offerings to their deaths.

**Personality**: Stoic, powerful, deeply traumatized. It takes the longest to trust but becomes an incredibly loyal partner once bonded. It hates confined spaces and the deep ritual sites.

**Appearance Progression**:
- Initially scarred and wary.
- With good care: Strong, almost regal, with small protective braids or tokens Rowan adds.
- Corrupted: Emaciated in places, eyes milky or burning, brands that spread like infection. Can become a terrifying “pale rider” horror.

**Abilities**:
- Overland speed and carrying capacity (changes how some zones are traversed)
- Charge / break obstacles (combat and puzzle utility)
- “Steady Presence” — reduces dread effects when Rowan is mounted or near it.
- Late game: Can carry two (Rowan + a badly injured companion) in escape sequences.

**Bond / Corruption**:
- Requires consistent care (food, grooming, protection from horror events). Neglect is very obvious and painful.
- This is the companion whose corruption or loss can feel the most like a betrayal of the “freedom” theme.

**Thematic Role**: The dream of escape and a future. The one that represents “we could just leave all of this behind.” The most tragic when the cycle catches up to it.

## Companion Interaction Rules (Design Notes)

- Companions have limited “endurance.” Forcing them into too many horror or combat situations without care causes bond damage + corruption gain.
- They remember. Specific choices (especially ones involving other children or previous offerings) will trigger unique dialogue or refusal scenes later.
- In the final act, companions can be used in “sacrificial” solutions. Some endings require choosing which one (if any) to give to the Hunger to save the rest.
- Positive playthroughs should feel warm and heartbreaking at the same time. Negative playthroughs should feel powerful but increasingly lonely and monstrous.
- Art pipeline note: Each companion needs at least pup/adult + corrupted variants. Bond level can be shown through small visual details (collar Rowan made, cleaner fur, fearful posture, etc.).

**Dialogue Integration**:
Every major companion has a set of personal nodes that can be gated behind `$bond_X` and `$corruption_X` thresholds. They can also comment on Rowan’s morality and revelations in ways that either support or challenge the player’s choices.
