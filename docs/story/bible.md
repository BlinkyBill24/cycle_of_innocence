# Cycle of Innocence — Story Bible

**Working Title**: Cycle of Innocence  
**Genre/Tone**: 2D top-down action-adventure RPG. Blends the wonder and exploration of classic Zelda/Secret of Mana with the creeping dread, body horror, and psychological tension of Super Metroid, Silent Hill, and Alien. Conspiracy layers and escalating revelations in the style of Attack on Titan. Deep coming-of-age and life-sim progression like Fable, but filtered through horror and moral consequence.  
**Core Theme**: You were never meant to grow up. The world requires children to die so that adults can pretend to be safe. Your escape breaks the pattern — and the pattern will do anything to correct itself. The only creatures that ever showed you mercy were animals. The only family you will ever have might be the ones you raise... or corrupt.

**Length Target (v1)**: 6–10 hours focused campaign. High replayability through morality, companion fates, and 4 major endings + NG+ knowledge carry-over.

## Protagonist: Rowan

**True Name / "Vessel" Designation**: Subject-07 (the seventh "perfect" candidate in the current cycle).

**Background (The Escape)**:
Rowan is 9 years old at the start. They were selected as this generation's "Offering" during the Night of the Hollowing — a secret ritual held every 7–12 years when the "Hunger" beneath the village grows restless. Selection is random, like a lottery or based on a "Community Harmony Score" that parents (and families) accumulate through work, loyalty displays, volunteering, and other contributions to village life. High-scoring or "lucky" families are "honored" by having a child chosen. The ritual takes place at the village playground — a place children are supposed to feel safe and happy — at dusk, with colorful but slightly wrong decorations.

The "Playtime Guardians" (creepy clowns in bright but faded costumes, oversized stuffed animals with too many stitches, and living toys that move on their own) lead the chosen child through gentle games and songs while the elders chant softly. Something ancient "tastes" the offering through the toys and laughter that turns hollow.

This time, something went "wrong" (or right, depending on perspective):
- One of the handlers (a conflicted young "Clown" performer named Thorne, wearing a cracked smiling mask) hesitated during the final "hug" phase and loosened the toy restraints.
- A sudden unnatural fog or "mistake" in the rite (the toys stopped singing) allowed Rowan to slip away into the growing dark.
- As Rowan fled the playground, the village carried on as if nothing had happened — they believed the ritual had been successful. No one raised an alarm. The adults smiled and went home thinking "the Hunger is fed, all is well."
- In the chaos at the edge of the playground, Rowan found (or was found by) a small terrified hound pup that had also been brought as part of the "lesser offering." The pup's mother had been killed earlier that day. The two escaped together into the fringes.

This is the inciting incident. Rowan is now a living crack in the cycle. The village does not yet realize the escape happened. The Hunger was not satisfied. Over the following days and weeks, strange things begin to happen (crops wither in patches, the dead do not stay quiet, "monsters" appear more frequently). The villagers start to wonder why the Offering "failed" and begin escalating the lottery — sacrificing more and more children in frantic additional rituals. This creates even more monsters from the new failures, accelerating the horror.

Rowan, hiding in the fringes with Briar, begins to piece together that the village still thinks everything is fine… for now.

**Age Progression & Visual/Moral Changes** (tied to [[characters/protagonist]]):

- **Child (9–11)**: Small, big haunted eyes, simple rough tunic with ritual tear marks. Innocent but already carrying guilt for surviving when others didn't. Movement is light and quick but vulnerable.
- **Early Teen (13–15)**: Taller, leaner, eyes harder. Starts wearing scavenged cloaks or pieces of old "ward" symbols. Can choose to keep small kindnesses (flowers for graves, sharing food with animals) or become colder.
- **Late Teen / Young Adult (18–22)**: Full height, posture changes with morality. High kindness = still some light in the eyes, protective stance. High ruthlessness = scars (self-inflicted or from using companions as weapons), "marked" glowing veins or shadow under skin that animals react to.

**Morality System Impact**:
Morality is not simple good/evil. It is "How much of yourself are you willing to sacrifice to survive... and what are you willing to sacrifice others for?"

- Positive (Kind / Empath): Rowan tries to protect the innocent (including animals and the few humans who show doubt). Animals bond deeply and gain abilities. Some "monsters" can be calmed or even redeemed. Revelations hit harder emotionally.
- Neutral (Pragmatic): Survival first, but not cruel. Bonds are functional. Can still achieve the "best" endings but with more loss.
- Negative (Ruthless / Vessel): Rowan uses people and animals as tools. Faster combat power (companions can be forced into dangerous roles). Body horror spreads earlier to Rowan and companions. Darker dialogue options and unique "corrupted" abilities. High chance of tragic or horror endings for the animals.

Key early choice example: In the first village edge area, Rowan can steal food from a desperate family or leave some of their own scavenged supplies. This sets early morality and affects whether a later NPC recognizes them as "the one who showed mercy" or "the thief."

## The Cycle & The Hunger (Core Conspiracy)

The village (called "Hollowmere" or "The Still Village" in some tellings) has a pact older than memory.

Every few years, one or more children are selected via the Harmony Lottery (or low family score) and offered during the Night of the Hollowing at the village playground. The ritual feeds and calms an ancient entity buried deep beneath the land — "The Hunger" or "The First Hollow."

In exchange, the village is "protected":
- Crops grow (twisted but abundant).
- The dead stay quiet.
- Outsiders get lost in the woods or are driven mad before they can reach the village.
- The "monsters" (corrupted previous offerings) mostly stay in the deep woods and only occasionally take someone who wanders too far.

**Major Twists** (layered revelations that should feel like punches):

1. **The Monsters Are Us**: The "beasts" and "spirits" that haunt the woods and occasionally raid are the failed or "completed" previous sacrifices. Their bodies were changed by the Hunger. Their minds are fractured echoes of the children they once were. Some still clutch broken toys or wear faded clown ruffs from the playground ritual.

2. **The Elders Are the Previous Cycle**: Many of the current elders and "protectors" are not the original wardens. They are survivors or "successful" offerings from 20–40 years ago who were allowed (or forced) to return in twisted, aged forms after "serving their time." They perpetuate the cycle because they believe (or have been broken into believing) that it is the only way to keep the Hunger from consuming *everything*.

3. **Rowan Was Never Random**: Rowan's bloodline carries a rare trait — they are a "perfect vessel." One of their ancestors was the child who *almost* broke the cycle generations ago but was recaptured and used to strengthen the seal instead. The Hunger has been waiting specifically for someone like Rowan. The "mistake" in the ritual may not have been a mistake at all — it may have been the Hunger *letting* them escape so it could hunt a stronger meal later.

4. **The Animals Are Also Part of the Cycle**: The companions Rowan raises were never ordinary. Briar (the dog) and the others carry echoes of previous companions from past escaped children. The Hunger corrupts bonds. If Rowan is ruthless, the animals can become new vessels or weapons for the cycle. If Rowan is kind, they can become the key to truly breaking or transforming it. The final twist can involve one of the animals having to be sacrificed or choosing to sacrifice themselves.

**The "Protectors" Lie**: The elders tell the village that the offerings keep the monsters away and the land fertile. In truth, the cycle *creates* the monsters and the land's "fertility" is the Hunger slowly digesting the world from below.

## Animal Companions (Found Family & Horror)

These are not pets. They are the only real family Rowan will ever have. Their arcs are as important as Rowan's.

**Briar (The Hound — First Companion, Act 0 Escape)**:
- Rescued as a terrified, half-starved pup during the escape.
- Abilities: Tracking (find hidden paths or lies), digging (reveal buried truths or bodies), loyal melee assist that grows stronger with bond.
- Arc: Briar is the emotional heart. High kindness = Briar becomes brave and protective, even willing to face the Hunger. Low morality = Briar is used as a weapon, becomes aggressive, and can be corrupted into a monstrous version that Rowan may have to put down or that turns on the village (tragic or horrifying).
- Key choice: Early on, Rowan can share their tiny amount of food with Briar or keep it all for themselves when both are starving.

**Echo (The Bird — Mid Game, Act 1)**:
- A sharp-eyed raven or unusual raptor that starts following Rowan after they show mercy (or ruthlessness) in the woods.
- Abilities: Scouting ahead (warning of danger or revealing distant events), carrying small objects, distracting enemies, aerial puzzle solutions (dropping items on switches).
- More fragile. Loss or corruption hits very hard narratively.
- Arc: Echo represents knowledge and warning. It can overhear elder conversations or see things from above that force revelations earlier.

**Corruption & Bond Mechanics** (mechanical + narrative):
- Bond score per companion (tracked in PlayerData and dialogue variables: $bond_briar, $corruption_briar).
- Care actions (feed, soothe after dread events, protect in combat) increase bond.
- Using them selfishly or exposing them to the Hunger increases corruption.
- High corruption on a companion can unlock powerful but horrifying "corrupted abilities" at the cost of loyalty and story consequences (they may refuse commands, attack Rowan, or become new horror set pieces).
- In endings, companions can survive together with Rowan, be lost heroically, be corrupted and have to be stopped, or even "ascend" with Rowan in a transformed cycle.

## High-Level Act Structure (Child → Adult)

**Act 0: The Escape (Child, ~9–10) — "The Night Everything Broke"**
- Opening: The ritual at the playground (with creepy clowns and living toys). Failure due to Thorne's hesitation + the fog. Flight with Briar into the night.
- First zone: The playground at dusk turning to night, then the immediate fringes (horror tutorial — bright colors turning wrong, toy sounds becoming threatening, limited visibility as fog rolls in, first glimpse of a "monster" that still has a stuffed animal in its hand).
- Rowan hides near the village edge. The adults are smiling and going about their evening as if the ritual succeeded perfectly. No one is looking for Rowan. The village believes "the Offering was accepted, the Hunger is calm, all is well."
- First morality choice + first real bond moment with Briar (while hiding and overhearing normal village life).
- Strange things begin happening in the background (a crop patch dies overnight, a dog howls wrong). The villagers start whispering that maybe the lottery needs to be run again soon.
- Ends with Rowan and Briar forced to go on the run or hide in the fringes as the first extra "emergency" offerings begin. First hint that the village's "success" is a lie that is already cracking.

**Act 1: The Fringes (Early Teen, ~13–15) — "We Only Have Each Other"**
- Wider exploration of the cursed woods and abandoned outlying farms.
- Recruitment / meeting of second companion (Echo the bird).
- First "hunt" — either Rowan is hunted by village "wardens" or they hunt a monster together.
- Major early twist: Discovery of old ritual remains or a previous child's belongings that prove the cycle has happened before.
- Morality branches open: How do you treat the few humans you meet (a doubting young warden, a mad hermit who was once an offering)?
- Companion raising mechanics introduced properly.
- Ends with a larger revelation or a direct confrontation that forces Rowan to leave the immediate area and go deeper (or return to the village in disguise).

**Act 2: The Heart of the Lie (Late Teen, ~17–18) — "Everyone Was Lying"**
- Return to or infiltration of Hollowmere and the deeper ritual sites.
- Major twists 2 & 3 land (monsters = previous children, elders = previous "successes").
- Loyalty crises (Briar or Echo may be badly corrupted or captured depending on choices). *(The third companion / mount was cut 2026-06-21 — see [[decisions/2026-06-21-companions-and-flute-gate]].)*
- Body horror escalates — Rowan or companions start showing physical changes based on morality and exposure.
- Found family vs blood family conflict (possible living relatives who either try to "save" Rowan by completing the ritual or reveal they always knew).
- Climactic choice point that determines the final path.

**Act 3: The Hollowing (Young Adult, 20+) — "What Will You Become?"**
- The deepest ritual site / the Hunger itself.
- Full truth about Rowan's bloodline and the animals' true nature.
- Final confrontations with the current "wardens" and possibly a corrupted version of a previous companion or even a twisted elder who was once kind.
- The choice: Break it (costly), Transform it (risky hope), Perpetuate it (power at the price of everything), or be broken by it.
- Multiple endings with different fates for Rowan and each companion.
- Epilogue that changes based on morality, which companions lived, and what was revealed.
